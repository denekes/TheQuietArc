Strategic M&A Perspectives

The Quiet Arc from Partnership to Purchase

Why the best acquisitions don't start with an offer. They start with something harder to manufacture and impossible to fake.

Daniel Enekes · SVP Strategic Partnerships & M&A
2026
Scroll

Most deals do not start with a data room. They start with people.

A handshake at a conference. A warm intro that almost didn't happen. A pilot project that quietly outperformed. The best acquisitions I've been part of didn't begin with a banker's pitch book. They began the way most meaningful relationships do: slowly, and with no guarantee of where they were headed.

01 — The Continuum

Five Stages, One Journey

Strategic partnerships, embedded relationships, minority investments, acquiring a subsidiary, and fully integrated M&A are not five different games. They are a single intertwined journey with different levels of commitment. The mistake most buyers make is treating them as separate decision trees. They're not. They're escalation points on the same path, and the quality of your behavior at each stage determines whether the next stage is even available to you.

Partnership Embedded Minority Stake Subsidiary Full M&A

The hard part is not the spreadsheet. It is winning enough trust that the other side would choose you over every other alternative, including doing nothing. And "doing nothing" is the most underestimated competitor in every deal. It's familiar, it's safe, and it doesn't require explaining anything to anyone.

Money helps. But many great companies are not for sale. Even when they are, they do not have to sell to you.

02 — The Paradox

The Best Companies Are the Hardest to Buy

There's a structural irony at the heart of M&A that nobody talks about enough: the companies most worth acquiring are the ones least likely to sell. They have cash flow, they have optionality, they have founders who still believe the best chapter hasn't been written yet. They're not sitting around waiting for your call.

The companies that are easy to buy? They're often easy for a reason. Declining growth. Exhausted founders. A cap table that needs an exit. The deal might close, but you inherit the decay.

This is the adverse selection problem of corporate development. If you only pursue companies that are actively shopping themselves, you systematically filter for the wrong targets. The real opportunities come from companies that aren't for sale, which means the entire playbook shifts from negotiation to courtship. You don't convince them with a term sheet. You earn the right to even present one.

The goal isn't to find a willing seller. It's to become the only buyer a reluctant one would consider.

Trust compounds faster than capital, but it collapses instantly when one of those pillars cracks.

03 — Trust Architecture

Trust Is a System, Not a Vibe

Trust isn't magic. It's mechanics. The organizational behavior research is clear: people trust you when they see three things simultaneously: ability, benevolence, and integrity. In deal-making, I translate that into three C's:

Pillar I
Competence
Pillar II
Care
Pillar III
Consistency

Competence means the other side believes you actually know how to run what you're buying. This is where most acquirers fail without realizing it. A founder hears your integration pitch and thinks: "These people don't understand what makes us work."

Care means they believe you're invested in outcomes beyond the financial model. Do you know their lead engineer's name? Do you know why they turned down the last acquisition offer? Have you asked what they're afraid of?

Consistency is the hardest one. It means behaving the same way in the boardroom as you do in the parking lot. It means your Tuesday follow-through matches your Monday enthusiasm. Founders are expert pattern-matchers. They've been pitched by investors, partners, and enterprise buyers their entire career. They can smell inconsistency across three time zones.

Miss any one of these, and no valuation model or price premium will save you.

04 — Signal Intelligence

The Data Room Nobody Opens

The real due diligence starts long before anyone signs an NDA. It happens in the micro-signals: how fast they return your email, who they bring to the second meeting, whether they share a problem unprompted, and what they ask you about your company. Every one of those moments is information.

Signal They're Leaning In They're Pulling Back
Meeting attendees Bring their CTO or head of product Send a junior BD person
Information flow Share a roadmap or internal challenge unprompted Deflect every specific question
Response latency Reply within 24 hours, ask follow-ups Go dark for weeks, then resurface vaguely
Questions they ask "How does your team handle X?" "Can you just send over the terms?"
NDA behavior Negotiate terms, then sign Refuse to engage or stall indefinitely

The best dealmakers I know spend more time reading these signals than they spend reading financials. By the time you're in the data room, the emotional decision is already 80% made. The numbers are confirmation, not revelation.

05 — Behavioral Economics

Why Money Is Not Enough

Owners are not pricing math problems. They live inside powerful human biases. Deals break not because the numbers don't add up, but because the psychology doesn't align.

Endowment Effect

Once people own something, they see it as part of themselves and value it far beyond market logic. A seller's willingness to accept often dwarfs a buyer's willingness to pay. This gap is not irrational. It is deeply human.

Status Quo Bias

The current path always feels safer than the unknown. Even a "better" deal can look risky if it threatens stability. Unless you lower the perceived risk of change, the default wins every time. Your job is to make the future feel less uncertain than the present.

Psychological Ownership

Founders don't just own shares. They identify with the company. Their name, their story, their legacy are woven into it. Challenge that identity and the deal freezes. Respect it, and you unlock cooperation money can't buy.

But there's a fourth bias that almost never gets discussed in M&A circles: loss aversion asymmetry. The pain a founder feels imagining their team being reorganized is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure they feel from the payout. This is why earn-outs so often fail. You're asking someone to trade a certain emotional loss for an uncertain financial gain. The math can be flawless and the psychology still catastrophic.

“

Deals aren't made in Excel. They're made in the space between logic and identity — and money alone doesn't reach that space.

06 — The Other Side of the Table

What Founders Hear When You Say "Acquisition"

If you're a buyer, this section is the most important one for you to internalize. If you're a founder, this might be the first time someone has described what you've been feeling.

When a founder hears the word "acquisition," they don't hear "liquidity event." They hear a series of existential questions: Will my team be safe? Will my product survive the next reorg? Will I become a line item in someone else's strategy deck? Will the thing I built still exist in three years?

The identity crisis is real. Founders spend years telling themselves and the world that their company is different, that it matters, that it's going to change something. Selling can feel like admitting the story is over. The most emotionally intelligent buyers understand this and reframe the conversation. It's not "we're buying your company." It's "we want to give your vision a bigger stage."

And here's a nuance that surprises people: the buyer who makes a founder uncomfortable by asking hard questions often earns more trust than the one who flatters. Why? Because a hard question signals that you've done the work. It signals you take the company seriously enough to challenge it. Flattery is cheap. Rigor is respect.

The founder isn't asking "Is this a fair price?" They're asking "Will you be a worthy steward of what I built?"
07 — BATNA

A Two-Sided Deal

Every deal is two-sided. You're buying, but you're also selling: selling yourself as the best future for their people, their product, and their purpose.

The other side isn't comparing your story to your spreadsheet. They're comparing it to their alternatives. That's the essence of BATNA: the Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement. Your offer has to outperform their best option, and sometimes your own.

Here's what most buyers forget: the founder's BATNA is almost always "keep running the company." That alternative has enormous emotional value. It's familiar, it's autonomous, and it doesn't require explaining to a board why you sold for less than the last 409A. To beat that BATNA, you can't just win the spreadsheet. You have to win the narrative.

The deal isn't just about what you can pay. It's about who they believe you'll be after the ink dries.
08 — The Danger Zone

The No-Man's Land Between Partnership and Purchase

There's a phase in many strategic relationships that nobody names but everyone recognizes. You've outgrown the partnership. The pilot worked. You're sharing roadmaps, aligning product strategy, maybe even co-selling. But nobody has said the word "acquisition" out loud. This is the no-man's land, and it's where most promising relationships go to die.

It dies because of ambiguity. The buyer thinks, "If we push too hard, we'll scare them off." The founder thinks, "If they wanted to buy us, they would have said so." Both sides wait. Both sides misread patience for disinterest. And eventually, a competitor with less tact but more directness makes the offer.

The remedy is naming the phase without forcing the outcome. Language like "We believe there's a deeper strategic relationship here, and we'd like to explore what that could look like" does the work. It opens the door without walking through it. It signals intent without creating pressure. And critically, it gives the founder permission to start processing the idea emotionally, which they need time to do. Ambiguity is not strategy. It's avoidance dressed in professionalism.

09 — Tempo

Time as a Strategic Weapon

The best acquirers understand something that auction-driven dealmaking never teaches: patience is a competitive advantage. Not passive patience. Not waiting for something to happen. Active patience: continuing to invest in the relationship, deepening trust, increasing your information advantage, and improving your position while the clock works in your favor.

Companies change. Founders' lives change. A founder who was allergic to acquisition in Q1 might be exhausted by Q4. A company that was thriving in March might miss a product cycle by October. If you've been a consistent, trusted presence throughout those changes, you're not just an option. You're the obvious option.

Conversely, urgency is almost always the enemy of good deals. When someone is in a rush to close, ask why. When you feel the urge to accelerate, examine it. Speed creates pressure, and pressure creates mistakes: overpaying, under-diligencing, or agreeing to structures you'll regret in eighteen months.

The deal you close in a week is rarely as good as the deal you've been building for a year.
10 — Discipline

The Willingness to Walk Away

You love the company. You've built trust, invested time, and you see the potential. But sometimes, you can't bridge certain gaps: valuation, structure, governance, or your redlines.

Walking away is one of the most powerful and painful tools in deal-making. It protects you from forcing a bad deal that could drain your focus, your capital, and your integrity. It also creates space for powerful comebacks and unexpected wins.

But here's what I've learned that isn't in any textbook: walking away also teaches you what you actually value. In the heat of a deal, everything feels critical. The moment you step back, you discover which elements were real requirements and which were ego. Some of the most valuable strategic clarity I've ever gained came not from closing a deal, but from choosing not to.

Every one of my best deals, I walked away at least once. That moment of silence, when you're ready to let go, often speaks louder than any term sheet. And sometimes, what comes back after the silence is better than what was on the table before.

11 — Day Two

The Second Sale

Closing is not the finish line. It's halftime. The day the deal closes, you haven't bought a company. You've bought the right to earn the loyalty of every person in it, starting from zero.

The engineers didn't choose to work for you. The product managers didn't sign up for your roadmap. The customers didn't renew because of your brand. Every single relationship that made the acquisition valuable is now in play, and none of them are contractually obligated to stay enthusiastic.

What the Team Is Thinking

"Is my manager going to survive this? Will my project get killed? Am I going to be the redundancy in a synergy slide? Should I start interviewing?" These questions begin the day the deal leaks, not the day it closes. Your first 90 days either confirm or deny their worst fears.

What the Customers Are Thinking

"Will the product I rely on get deprioritized? Will the people I trust still be there? Is this going to become another bloated enterprise product that loses its soul?" Answer these questions before they ask. Silence is not reassurance. Silence is confirmation of their fears.

The companies that get acquisition integration right treat it as a second founding, not a migration project. They send the CEO to the acquired team's office in week one. They protect the acquired product's roadmap for the first year. They promote someone from the acquired team into a visible leadership role. These are not gestures. They are strategic signals that determine whether you actually capture the value you paid for.

12 — Calibration

The Integration Tabasco

Integration is like Tabasco: use just enough to bring out the flavor, not drown it. Decide integration by value, not by habit.

Separate subsidiary
Shared systems
Over-integration ⚠

Keep it a separate subsidiary when speed, brand, and talent are the moat. Integrate only where shared systems truly create advantage: billing, infrastructure, go-to-market motion. The right dose accelerates growth. Too much burns what made the deal worth doing in the first place.

The question I ask to calibrate: "If we integrate this function, does it make the acquired company faster or slower?" If the answer is slower, leave it alone. Optimization is not the goal. Acceleration is.
